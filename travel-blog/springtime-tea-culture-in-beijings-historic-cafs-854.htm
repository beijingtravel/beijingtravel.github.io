Springtime Tea Culture in Beijing’s Historic Cafés

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

As the winter chill fades and the first cherry blossoms appear, Beijing’s historic cafés come alive with a unique blend of tradition and modernity. Spring is the perfect time to explore the city’s tea culture, where centuries-old teahouses sit alongside trendy coffee shops, offering a fascinating glimpse into Beijing’s evolving culinary landscape. Whether you’re a tea connoisseur or a casual traveler, these spots provide an unforgettable experience.

The Charm of Beijing’s Historic Cafés

Beijing’s historic cafés are more than just places to drink tea—they’re cultural landmarks. Many of these establishments are housed in traditional siheyuan (courtyard homes) or repurposed colonial buildings, creating an atmosphere that transports visitors back in time. The fusion of Chinese tea rituals with Western-style café culture makes these spaces particularly appealing to both locals and tourists.

Must-Visit Historic Cafés

  1. Lao She Teahouse
    Named after the famous Chinese writer Lao She, this teahouse is a tribute to old Beijing’s literary and artistic heritage. In spring, they serve delicate floral teas like jasmine and osmanthus, paired with traditional snacks like sweet pea cakes. The teahouse also hosts Peking opera performances, adding a theatrical touch to your tea experience.

  2. Capital M (Formerly Mei Lanfang’s Residence)
    Once the home of Peking opera legend Mei Lanfang, this elegant café now serves high tea with a Chinese twist. Their spring menu features matcha-infused pastries and chrysanthemum tea, a refreshing choice for the season.

  3. Forbidden City Corner Tower Café
    Located near the Forbidden City, this café offers stunning views of ancient architecture. Their signature "Imperial Spring Tea Set" includes rare white teas and handcrafted dim sum, making it a favorite among history buffs.

The Art of Spring Tea Pairings

Spring teas are lighter and more aromatic than their winter counterparts. Beijing’s cafés take full advantage of seasonal ingredients, creating pairings that delight the senses.

Popular Spring Tea Varieties

  • Longjing (Dragon Well) Tea – A delicate green tea with a nutty flavor, often served with almond cookies.
  • Bi Luo Chun – Known for its floral notes, this tea pairs beautifully with honey-glazed pastries.
  • Jasmine Silver Needle – A fragrant white tea that complements light desserts like mango pudding.

Café-Exclusive Blends

Many historic cafés craft their own signature blends. For example, The Red Gate Café offers a "Spring in Beijing" blend, combining rose petals, lychee black tea, and a hint of bergamot—a nod to both Chinese and Western tea traditions.

Tea and Tourism: A Perfect Match

Beijing’s tea culture isn’t just about drinking—it’s an immersive experience. Several cafés offer tea ceremonies where visitors can learn the proper way to brew and appreciate tea. These sessions often include stories about the origins of different teas, making them a hit with curious travelers.

Tea-Themed Walking Tours

For those who want to dive deeper, guided walking tours explore Beijing’s historic tea districts, such as Maliandao (the city’s largest tea market). Along the way, stops at hidden teahouses provide tastings and insider knowledge about tea trading history.

Instagram-Worthy Tea Moments

Spring’s soft lighting and blooming flowers make Beijing’s cafés a paradise for photographers. The Courtyard Tea Garden in Houhai is particularly photogenic, with its willow trees and vintage tea sets arranged on wooden trays.

The Rise of Modern Tea Trends

While tradition reigns supreme, Beijing’s cafés are also embracing contemporary trends. Bubble tea shops now sit beside century-old teahouses, and fusion drinks like "oolong latte" are gaining popularity.

Tea Cocktails and Mixology

Some upscale cafés, like The Temple House, have introduced tea-infused cocktails. Their "Jasmine Gin Fizz" combines jasmine green tea with gin and elderflower, creating a refreshing spring drink.

Sustainability in Tea Culture

Eco-conscious cafés are promoting loose-leaf tea over single-use bags, and some even offer "tea meditation" sessions to encourage mindfulness—a growing trend among wellness-focused travelers.

Final Sip

From the first sip of a steaming cup of Longjing to the last bite of a rose-infused macaron, springtime in Beijing’s historic cafés is a journey through flavor, history, and culture. Whether you’re seeking tranquility in a hidden courtyard or the buzz of a modern tea bar, the city’s vibrant tea scene has something for everyone.
