How Much Cash to Carry in Beijing for a Stress-Free Trip

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Traveling to Beijing is an exciting adventure, but figuring out how much cash to carry can be tricky. While digital payments like WeChat Pay and Alipay dominate China’s financial landscape, cash still plays a role—especially for tourists. This guide breaks down the essentials, helping you budget wisely while enjoying everything from the Great Wall to Peking duck.

Why Cash Still Matters in Beijing

Despite China’s rapid shift toward mobile payments, cash remains useful in certain situations:
- Small vendors and street markets: Many hole-in-the-wall eateries and souvenir stalls prefer cash.
- Taxis and public transport: While ride-hailing apps like Didi accept digital payments, some older taxi drivers may not.
- Tipping culture: Though not common in China, cash is handy for tipping tour guides or hotel staff.
- Emergency backup: If your phone dies or your digital wallet malfunctions, cash saves the day.

How Much Cash Should You Carry Per Day?

Aim for ¥300–500 per person per day (roughly $40–70 USD) for a comfortable experience. Here’s how it breaks down:

1. Food and Drinks

  • Street food/snacks: ¥10–30 per item (e.g., jianbing, chuan’r).
  • Local restaurants: ¥50–150 per meal.
  • Upscale dining: ¥200+ per person (credit cards accepted, but cash works).

2. Transportation

  • Subway/bus: ¥3–10 per ride (cash or transit card).
  • Taxis: ¥30–100 for short trips (cash preferred in older cabs).

3. Attractions and Shopping

  • Temple of Heaven/Forbidden City: ¥40–60 entrance fee (cash or online tickets).
  • Hutong souvenirs: ¥20–100 for small items (cash negotiable).

Where to Exchange Currency

Avoid exchanging money at airports or hotels—their rates are poor. Instead:
- Bank of China: Reliable with fair rates.
- ATMs: Use international cards at major banks (check fees with your bank first).
- Authorized exchange counters: Look for them in shopping districts like Wangfujing.

Tips to Stretch Your Cash

  • Haggle in markets: Vendors often inflate prices for tourists.
  • Use public transport: It’s cheap and efficient.
  • Carry small bills: Breaking ¥100 notes can be hard at small shops.

When to Rely on Digital Payments

For larger expenses (hotels, chain stores), WeChat Pay/Alipay are king. Set these up before your trip:
- Link an international card or ask a Chinese friend to help top up your wallet.
- Scan QR codes seamlessly at 90% of businesses.

Final Safety Tips

  • Split cash: Keep some in your wallet, some in your hotel safe.
  • Beware of pickpockets in crowded areas like the Summer Palace or Sanlitun.
  • Notify your bank of travel plans to avoid card freezes.

With this balance of cash and digital savvy, you’ll navigate Beijing like a pro—stress-free and ready for adventure!
