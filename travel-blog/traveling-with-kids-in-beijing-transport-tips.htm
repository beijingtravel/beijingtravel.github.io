Traveling with Kids in Beijing: Transport Tips

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

The very idea of traveling to Beijing with kids can feel simultaneously thrilling and daunting. The Great Wall, the Forbidden City, the pandas... these are the dreams we sell our children. The reality we parents fret over? The mind-boggling scale of the city, the infamous traffic, and the sheer logistical puzzle of getting a tired, hungry, or suddenly-uncooperative small human from Point A to Point B without losing our sanity. Fear not, fellow adventurers. Beijing, despite its vastness, is one of the most transport-accessible megacities in the world. The key is knowing which key to use for which lock.

The Beijing Metro: Your Underground Lifeline

For most families, the Beijing Subway will be the backbone of your travel. It's fast, affordable, air-conditioned in summer, heated in winter, and blissfully immune to the gridlock above. But with over 20 lines weaving a complex web under the city, a little strategy goes a long way.

Mastering the Metro Map with Kids

Before you even descend into a station, download the official "Beijing Subway" app or use the map feature on Alipay. These provide real-time routing. Sit down with your older kids and show them the map—turn it into a game. "We're at the Tiananmen East station (the red rooster), and we need to get to the Olympic Green (the Bird's Nest!). Let's trace the line." This involvement makes them feel like co-navigators, not just dragged-along passengers.

Ticket Purchasing and Entry: Avoiding the Scramble

Forget fumbling for cash at the ticket machines. The absolute easiest way is to set up a transit card on your phone. Link the "Yikatong" (the Beijing transit card) to your Alipay or WeChat app. You simply scan your phone's QR code at the gate. For families, one phone can be used for multiple scans (you scan, go through, then scan again for your child). Alternatively, you can buy physical Yikatong cards at ticket windows—they require a deposit but are handy. Children under a certain height (typically 1.3 meters) ride for free, but you'll need to take them through the wide manual gate next to the turnstiles and show them to the attendant.

Surviving Rush Hour and Finding a Seat

Avoid the metro between 7:30-9:00 AM and 5:00-7:00 PM on weekdays if you can. If you can't, here's the plan: Stations with major interchanges are chaos. Try to board at the ends of the lines for a better chance at seats. When a train arrives, position yourself where the doors open, but stand to the side to let people off first—it's the local custom and prevents you from being bowled over. Backpacks worn on the front are a pro-move in crowded carriages.

Hailing Rides: The Comfort of Didi and Taxis

When little legs are done walking, or your destination isn't metro-adjacent, ride-hailing is your best friend.

Didi Chuxing: The Chinese Uber

Download the Didi app before you arrive and set it up with a payment method. It's a game-changer. You can input your destination precisely, see the fare upfront, and the driver will navigate directly there without needing verbal instructions. The options are key: * Didi Express: The standard, affordable option. * Premium: For a few more yuan, you get a nicer, more comfortable car—worth it after a long day. * Didi Taxi: Hails a regular metered taxi through the app. The best part? No language barrier. You can use the in-app translation feature to message the driver, and they can message you back when they arrive.

Flagging Down a Traditional Taxi

You can still hail a cab on the street, especially outside hotels and major attractions. Look for a taxi with a red "空车" (Vacant) light in the windshield. Have your destination written in Chinese characters on your phone—this is non-negotiable. Show the address to the driver before you get in. Ensure they use the meter. While most are honest, this simple step avoids any "special price" tourist scams.

Car Seats: The Great Dilemma

This is the biggest challenge for Western families. Car seats are virtually non-existent in Chinese taxis and ride-shares. It is a cultural and legal norm that is different from back home. Your options are: 1. Bring your own travel car seat. This is the safest option, but it's bulky to carry around all day if you're not going directly from your hotel. 2. Use a ride-hailing service that allows you to request a car seat. Didi has a "Baby Car" option, but availability is extremely limited and not guaranteed. 3. Rely on public transport for most trips and use taxis/Didi for very short, low-speed journeys when necessary, holding your child securely. This is a risk-assessment every parent must make for themselves.

The Iconic Beijing Experience: Cycling the Hutongs

No trip to Beijing is complete without exploring the ancient alleyways known as Hutongs. The best way to do this? On two wheels.

Bike-Sharing Revolution: Mobike and HelloBike

Beijing is flooded with bright orange, blue, and yellow shared bikes. Download the Mobike or HelloBike apps, scan a QR code, and you're off. They are incredibly cheap and perfect for short hops. Most have child seats or small wheel-attached trailers, but you must inspect them carefully for safety before use.

Ricksaw Tours: Let Someone Else Do the Pedaling

For a more guided and less strenuous tour, a pedal-rickshaw ride through the Hutongs is fantastic fun for kids. You can negotiate a price for a 30-minute or hour-long tour. The drivers often serve as informal guides, pointing out historical points of interest. Agree on the price and duration clearly before you set off.

Conquering the Great Wall: The Long-Distance Trek

Visiting the Great Wall is a pinnacle of any Beijing trip, but it's also the trickiest transport puzzle. The key sections for families are Badaling (easiest to access, but most crowded) and Mutianyu (still accessible, slightly less crowded, with a toboggan ride down!).

Organized Tours vs. DIY

Organized Tours: The simplest, most brainless option. A bus picks you up and drops you off. The downside is you're on their schedule, which can be tough with naptimes. DIY by Train: For Badaling, you can take a public train from Huangtudian Station. It's scenic and cheap, but you have to get yourself to the suburban station. DIY by Private Car: The ultimate flexibility. Book a private car with a driver for the day through your hotel or a service like Trip.com. It's more expensive, but for a family of four, it can be worth its weight in gold. You leave when you want, come back when the kids hit their wall (pun intended), and the car can wait for you.

Navigating Airports and Train Stations

Beijing Capital International (PEK) & Daxing (PKX)

Both major airports are far from the city center. The Airport Express train is your most reliable bet to avoid traffic. It connects seamlessly to the metro network. Taxis and Didi are also available, but the journey can take over an hour if traffic is heavy.

Bullet Traps: Beijing's High-Speed Rail Stations

Traveling to Xi'an or Shanghai? Beijing's main stations (Beijing South, Beijing West) are cities in themselves. Arrive at least an hour before your train. The high-speed trains themselves are a highlight for kids—smooth, incredibly fast, and spotlessly clean. Pack snacks, as the food options on board are limited. Enjoy the ride; it's a comfortable and fascinating way to see the countryside.

Embrace the chaos, plan ahead, and be flexible. Your transport adventures in Beijing will become some of the most memorable parts of your family's story.
