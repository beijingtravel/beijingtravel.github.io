How to Get a Test in Beijing if You Develop Symptoms

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

The iconic red gates of the Forbidden City, the sprawling majesty of the Great Wall, the bustling, ancient alleys of the hutongs—Beijing is a traveler’s dream. You’ve planned this trip for months, mapping out every temple and tasting every imaginable Peking duck. But then, it happens. A tickle in your throat, a slight fever, a wave of fatigue that feels heavier than just jet lag. In that moment, the excitement of exploration can quickly be overshadowed by a very practical and urgent question: What do I do now?

Navigating healthcare in a foreign country, especially when you're feeling unwell, can be daunting. But in a global city like Beijing, the system for handling potential COVID-19 cases is well-established, efficient, and accessible to foreigners. This guide is your practical travel companion, designed to walk you through the steps calmly and clearly, so you can get the care you need and get back to enjoying your adventure as soon as possible.

Step 1: Don't Panic, Assess and Isolate

The first and most important step is to stay calm. Developing mild cold-like symptoms does not automatically mean you have COVID-19. It could be a simple reaction to pollution, air conditioning, fatigue, or a common seasonal bug.

Recognizing the Symptoms

Be aware of the common symptoms that would warrant getting a test. These include, but are not limited to: * Fever (a temperature of 37.3°C / 99.1°F or higher) * Dry cough * Fatigue * Loss of smell or taste * Sore throat * Runny nose or congestion

Your Immediate Action Plan

As soon as you suspect you might be ill, your priority is to protect others and prevent any potential spread. * Self-Isolate: Return to and stay in your hotel room. Avoid all common areas like the lobby, restaurant, gym, and pool. * Inform Your Hotel: Call the front desk immediately. Reputable hotels in Beijing have strict protocols for this exact situation. They can assist you with getting food delivered to your room, provide you with extra supplies like water and tissues, and guide you on the next steps. They will also be able to inform other guests and staff discreetly and professionally. * Wear a Mask: If you absolutely must leave your room (for instance, to go to a testing site), wear a high-quality mask (like a KN95 or N95) at all times.

Step 2: The Pathways to Getting Tested

In Beijing, there are several ways to get a test. The best choice for a tourist will depend on the severity of your symptoms and your location.

Option A: The Designated Fever Clinic (Fàshāo Ménzhěn 发热门诊)

This is the most direct and recommended route if your symptoms are pronounced, especially if you have a clear fever.

  • What it is: Fever Clinics are specialized departments within hospitals specifically designed to screen and treat patients with fevers and respiratory symptoms. They are equipped to handle potential COVID-19 cases safely.
  • How to Find One: Use the Amap (高德地图) or Baidu Maps (百度地图) apps on your phone. Simply type "Fever Clinic" or "发热门诊" into the search bar. The app will show you the nearest options. Your hotel concierge can also provide you with the address of the closest and most foreigner-friendly one.
  • What to Expect: Upon arrival, you will be guided to a separate, isolated area. The process is streamlined:
    1. Registration: You will need to show your passport and provide your personal information and travel history.
    2. Temperature Check & Triage: A nurse will check your temperature and assess your symptoms.
    3. The Test: You will most likely be given a nucleic acid test (NAT or PCR test), which involves a deep nasal or throat swab. The process is quick, though it can be slightly uncomfortable.
    4. Waiting for Results: This is the critical part. You will typically be required to wait in a designated isolation area within the clinic until your results come back, which can take anywhere from 4 to 8 hours. Do not expect to be able to leave. Come prepared with a fully charged phone and a power bank.

Option B: Community Health Service Centers

For milder symptoms without a fever, a local community health center might offer testing. However, their capacity for handling foreigners and their operating hours can be more variable. This option is often better for those who live in the city rather than tourists. It's generally safer to head to a Fever Clinic or a large hospital.

Option C: Private Testing for Travel (Not for Symptomatic Cases)

You will see many private clinics and testing booths around the city that offer PCR tests for travel purposes. It is crucial to understand that you cannot and should not go to one of these if you have symptoms. They are for asymptomatic individuals only. If you report symptoms, they will be required to turn you away and direct you to a Fever Clinic. Attempting to hide your symptoms is irresponsible and could have serious legal consequences.

Step 3: Navigating the Healthcare System as a Traveler

This is often the part that worries travelers the most, but with a little preparation, it is manageable.

Language is Your Biggest Hurdle (and How to Overcome It)

Do not assume that medical staff will speak fluent English. While doctors at major international hospitals might, staff at public Fever Clinics likely will not. * Use a Translation App: Apps like Google Translate (with its camera function for instant translation of documents) and Microsoft Translator are invaluable. Download the Chinese language pack for offline use. * Prepare Key Phrases: Have these written down or saved on your phone: * "I have a fever / cough / sore throat." (Wǒ fāshāo / késou / hóulóng tòng. 我发烧 / 咳嗽 / 喉咙痛。) * "I am a tourist." (Wǒ shì lǚyóu de. 我是旅游的。) * "My passport number is ______." * "I need a COVID-19 test." (Wǒ xūyào zuò hésuān jiǎncè. 我需要做核酸检测。) * Ask Your Hotel for Help: The most effective solution is to ask your hotel if a staff member who speaks English can assist you over the phone while you are at the clinic. They can act as a translator between you and the medical staff.

Costs and Payment

Healthcare in China is not free for foreigners. You should be prepared to pay out-of-pocket for the consultation and the test. The cost for a PCR test at a public hospital is typically reasonable, often ranging from 60 to 150 RMB (approximately $8-$22 USD). Major international hospitals will be significantly more expensive. Ensure you have a Chinese payment app like Alipay or WeChat Pay set up on your phone with a linked credit card, as cash is often not accepted in these settings.

Step 4: The "What If" Scenarios

It's essential to be mentally prepared for all possible outcomes of your test.

Scenario 1: The Test is Negative

Congratulations! This is the best-case scenario and by far the most common outcome. The hospital will provide you with a negative test result report, which you should keep. You will be free to leave and resume your travels. You may still feel unwell, so it's wise to take it easy for a day or two, rest in your hotel, and stay hydrated. You can explore local delivery apps like Meituan (美团) or Ele.me (饿了么) to have soup, tea, and other comforting foods brought directly to your door.

Scenario 2: The Test is Positive

If your test comes back positive, Beijing's public health protocol will be activated immediately. While policies can change, the general process has been as follows: * Isolation and Observation: You will not be free to leave. You will be transferred to a designated government isolation facility or hospital for observation and treatment. These are not five-star hotels; they are functional medical facilities. * What to Expect in Isolation: You will be provided with meals and basic medical care. The length of your stay will depend on your symptoms and subsequent test results. You typically need to test negative twice in a row over a period of days before being discharged. * Informing Your Contacts: The health authorities will conduct contact tracing. They will likely ask you for a detailed list of where you have been and who you have had close contact with. It is crucial to be honest and cooperative. You should also inform your home country's embassy or consulate in Beijing.

Before You Travel: Your Pre-Trip Health Kit

The best way to deal with a health scare is to prevent it or be thoroughly prepared.

  • Travel Insurance: This is non-negotiable. Ensure your travel insurance policy has comprehensive medical coverage, including specific provisions for COVID-19 quarantine. Check if it covers costs for isolation stays and trip interruptions. Carry your insurance card and the emergency contact number with you at all times.
  • Digital Preparedness: Before you leave home, download Alipay or WeChat and link an international credit card. Download Amap and Baidu Maps. Install a reliable VPN if you need access to websites like Google and its services (including Translate and Maps), as they are restricted in China.
  • Embassy Information: Save the address and contact number of your country's embassy in Beijing in your phone and write it down on a physical card.
  • A Basic Health Kit: Pack your own thermometer, a good supply of high-quality masks, rapid antigen tests (if allowed by customs), and your regular medications.

Getting sick on vacation is never part of the plan, but in a city as vast and organized as Beijing, the system is designed to handle it. By knowing what to do, staying calm, and being prepared, you can navigate the situation safely and efficiently. The Terracotta Warriors and the Summer Palace will still be there waiting for you once you've recovered. Your health and the safety of those around you are the most important sights to see to.
