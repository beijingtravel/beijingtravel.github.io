Beijing Travel Plan: How to Visit the Lama Temple

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Beijing is a city where ancient traditions blend seamlessly with modern energy. Among its many historical gems, the Lama Temple (Yonghegong) stands out as one of the most spiritually significant and visually stunning sites. Whether you're a history buff, a culture enthusiast, or simply looking for a peaceful escape from the city's hustle, this Tibetan Buddhist temple is a must-visit. Here’s everything you need to know to plan your trip.

Why Visit the Lama Temple?

The Lama Temple, officially known as Yonghegong, is not just another tourist attraction—it’s a living center of Tibetan Buddhism in the heart of Beijing. Built in 1694 as a residence for Emperor Yongzheng before being converted into a lamasery, the temple is now a vibrant place of worship and a treasure trove of Buddhist art.

Key Highlights:

  • The Giant Buddha Statue: The 18-meter-tall Maitreya Buddha, carved from a single sandalwood tree, is a jaw-dropping masterpiece.
  • Architectural Grandeur: A mix of Han Chinese and Tibetan styles, with golden roofs, intricate carvings, and colorful murals.
  • Spiritual Atmosphere: Witness monks chanting, devotees offering incense, and the scent of sandalwood filling the air.

Best Time to Visit

To make the most of your visit, timing is key.

Ideal Seasons:

  • Spring (April-May): Pleasant weather, fewer crowds, and blooming flowers in the temple courtyards.
  • Autumn (September-October): Crisp air and golden hues create a serene ambiance.

Best Time of Day:

  • Early Morning (8:00 AM - 10:00 AM): Beat the tour groups and experience the temple at its most tranquil.
  • Late Afternoon (3:00 PM - 5:00 PM): Soft lighting enhances the temple’s golden details.

Avoid visiting during major Chinese holidays like Golden Week (early October) when crowds can be overwhelming.

How to Get There

Located in Beijing’s Dongcheng District, the Lama Temple is easily accessible via public transport.

By Subway:

Take Line 2 or Line 5 to Yonghegong Station. Exit via Exit C (South) and walk south for 2 minutes—you’ll spot the temple’s striking red gates.

By Taxi or Ride-Hailing:

Simply tell the driver “Yonghegong” (雍和宫). Most drivers will recognize the name.

On Foot or Bike:

If you’re staying nearby (like in the hutongs of Gulou), consider a leisurely walk or bike ride through Beijing’s historic alleyways.

Tickets and Practical Tips

Ticket Information:

  • Cost: ¥25 (about $3.50) per person.
  • Where to Buy: Tickets can be purchased at the entrance or via official WeChat mini-programs (scan the QR code on-site).
  • Free Audio Guides: Available in multiple languages—just leave a deposit.

Pro Tips:

  • Dress Modestly: This is an active place of worship, so avoid shorts, tank tops, or revealing clothing.
  • No Flash Photography: Respect the sacred atmosphere.
  • Incense Etiquette: If you choose to burn incense (often included with your ticket), hold it with both hands and bow three times.

Exploring the Temple: A Step-by-Step Guide

The Lama Temple is laid out along a central axis, with five main halls. Here’s how to navigate them like a pro.

1. The Gate of Harmony (Yonghemen)

Start your journey here, where you’ll get your first glimpse of the temple’s grandeur. Notice the guardian statues on either side—they’re said to ward off evil spirits.

2. The Hall of Heavenly Kings (Tianwangdian)

This hall houses the Four Heavenly Kings, fierce protectors of Buddhist teachings. Look up to see the magnificent Maitreya Buddha statue smiling down at visitors.

3. The Hall of Harmony and Peace (Yonghegong)

The main hall features three giant bronze Buddha statues representing the past, present, and future. The intricate murals here depict scenes from Buddha’s life.

4. The Hall of Eternal Blessing (Yongyoudian)

Dedicated to the Medicine Buddha, this hall is where many locals pray for health and longevity.

5. The Pavilion of Ten Thousand Happinesses (Wanfuge)

The grand finale—home to the 26-meter sandalwood Buddha. The sheer scale and craftsmanship will leave you in awe.

Nearby Attractions to Pair With Your Visit

Make a day of it by exploring these nearby spots:

Confucius Temple and Imperial College

Just a 5-minute walk from the Lama Temple, this serene complex was once China’s highest academic institution. The ancient cypress trees and stone steles add to the scholarly vibe.

Hutongs of Gulou

Wander through Beijing’s iconic alleyways, where traditional courtyard homes (siheyuan) house trendy cafes, indie shops, and hidden bars.

Ghost Street (Guijie)

A foodie paradise, especially at night. Try spicy crayfish, Peking duck, or lamb skewers under the neon lights.

Cultural Insights: Understanding Tibetan Buddhism

To appreciate the Lama Temple fully, here’s some context:

The Role of the Dalai Lama

The temple has strong ties to Tibet. The current Dalai Lama once studied here, and the temple remains a symbol of cultural exchange between Han and Tibetan traditions.

Prayer Wheels and Mantras

You’ll see devotees spinning prayer wheels—a practice believed to spread blessings. The wheels are inscribed with the mantra “Om Mani Padme Hum.”

Food and Souvenirs

What to Eat Nearby

  • Vegetarian Buddhist Cuisine: Try meat-free dishes at King’s Joy (a Michelin-starred restaurant nearby).
  • Traditional Snacks: Sample zhima shaobing (sesame cakes) from street vendors.

Souvenir Ideas

  • Prayer Beads: Sold at temple shops, these make meaningful keepsakes.
  • Thangka Art: Intricate Tibetan Buddhist scroll paintings are available in nearby galleries.

Final Travel Hacks

  • Combine with a Guided Tour: Companies like Lost Plate offer food and culture tours that include the Lama Temple.
  • Visit on the 1st or 15th of the Lunar Month: These are especially auspicious days, with more ceremonies and devotees.
  • Learn a Few Mandarin Phrases: Simple greetings like “Nǐ hǎo” (Hello) or “Xièxiè” (Thank you) go a long way.

The Lama Temple is more than just a sightseeing stop—it’s a gateway to Beijing’s spiritual soul. With this guide, you’re ready to explore its golden halls, hidden stories, and tranquil corners like a true traveler.
