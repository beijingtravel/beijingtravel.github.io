How to Stay Safe in Beijing with Children

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Beijing is a vibrant city filled with rich history, modern attractions, and endless opportunities for family adventures. However, traveling with children requires extra planning to ensure their safety and comfort. From navigating crowded tourist spots to handling unexpected situations, here’s your ultimate guide to keeping your little ones safe while exploring Beijing.

Understanding Beijing’s Environment

Air Quality and Weather Considerations

Beijing’s air quality can fluctuate, especially during winter when pollution levels may rise. Before heading out, check apps like AQI China or AirVisual to monitor real-time air quality. If the AQI is high (above 150), consider:
- Packing N95 masks for kids (small sizes are available in pharmacies).
- Limiting outdoor activities or opting for indoor attractions like the National Museum of China or Beijing Aquarium.
- Using air purifiers in your hotel room.

Weather extremes are another factor. Summers can be scorching (over 35°C/95°F), while winters drop below freezing. Dress children in layers and carry sunscreen, hats, and reusable water bottles.

Navigating Crowds Safely

Popular spots like the Forbidden City, Tiananmen Square, and Summer Palace get extremely crowded. To avoid losing sight of your child:
- Use bright-colored clothing or matching family outfits for easy spotting.
- Teach older kids to memorize your phone number or hotel address.
- Consider child leashes or wristbands for toddlers in packed areas.

Transportation Tips for Families

Public Transport vs. Private Options

Beijing’s subway is efficient but can be overwhelming during rush hours (7–9 AM and 5–7 PM). For families:
- Avoid subways with strollers unless using elevators (not all stations have them).
- Opt for taxis or ride-hailing apps like Didi (China’s Uber). Request child car seats in advance—though availability isn’t guaranteed.

Pedestrian Safety

Sidewalks near tourist areas are often crowded with bikes, e-scooters, and vendors. Hold younger children’s hands tightly and:
- Look both ways before crossing—even at green lights—as scooters sometimes ignore traffic rules.
- Use pedestrian bridges or underpasses near major roads like Chang’an Avenue.

Food and Water Safety

Choosing Child-Friendly Meals

Street food is tempting, but sensitive stomachs may struggle. Stick to:
- Reputable restaurants (check Dianping or TripAdvisor reviews).
- Mild dishes like jiaozi (dumplings) or chao fan (fried rice). Avoid overly spicy options.
- Bottled water only—tap water isn’t safe to drink.

Handling Food Allergies

If your child has allergies:
- Carry a translated card listing allergens in Chinese (e.g., “我的孩子对花生过敏” – “My child is allergic to peanuts”).
- Supermarkets like Jenny Lou’s or April Gourmet stock imported snacks for emergencies.

Health and Emergency Preparedness

Medical Facilities

International hospitals like United Family Healthcare or Peking Union Medical College Hospital have English-speaking staff. Keep these numbers saved:
- 120 for emergencies.
- Your country’s embassy contact (e.g., U.S. Embassy: +86-10-8531-3000).

Pharmacies and Essentials

Local pharmacies (药店, yaodian) sell basics, but bring:
- Pediatric fever reducers (e.g., ibuprofen).
- Motion sickness pills for long bus rides to the Great Wall.
- Hand sanitizer and wet wipes—public restrooms often lack soap.

Cultural Etiquette and Scams

Teaching Kids Cultural Sensitivity

  • Remind children not to point at people or statues (considered rude).
  • Practice quiet voices in temples like the Lama Temple.

Avoiding Common Scams

  • Tea House Scams: Strangers may invite you for tea, then demand exorbitant fees. Politely decline.
  • Fake Taxis: Use only metered cabs or Didi. Avoid unmarked cars.
  • Overpriced Souvenirs: Haggle at markets like Silk Street, but set a budget with kids beforehand.

Fun Yet Safe Attractions

Top Child-Friendly Spots

  1. Beijing Zoo: Visit early to see pandas before crowds arrive.
  2. Olympic Park: Rent bikes or scooters to explore the Bird’s Nest.
  3. 798 Art Zone: Interactive exhibits and open spaces for creative play.

Theme Parks and Indoor Fun

  • Happy Valley Beijing: Thrill rides for older kids; check height restrictions.
  • KidZania Beijing: Role-playing activities in a controlled environment.

Final Safety Hacks

  • WiFi and Communication: Rent a portable WiFi device or buy a local SIM card (China Mobile/China Unicom) to stay connected.
  • Lost Child Protocol: Designate a meeting spot (e.g., info booth) at each attraction.
  • Copies of Documents: Keep scanned passports and visas in your email or a secure cloud.

By blending preparation with flexibility, your family can enjoy Beijing’s wonders while minimizing risks. Every challenge becomes a shared story—whether it’s mastering the subway or bonding over Peking duck!
