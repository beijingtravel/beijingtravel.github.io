Beijing Travel: How to Visit the Great Wall Easily

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

The Great Wall of China is more than just a wall—it’s a legend, a symbol, and one of the most awe-inspiring human achievements in history. For many travelers visiting Beijing, a trip to the Great Wall is at the top of their bucket list. But between the crowds, the distance, and the various sections to choose from, planning a visit can feel overwhelming. The good news? With a little know-how, visiting the Great Wall can be easy, enjoyable, and absolutely unforgettable.

Why the Great Wall Should Be on Your List

Stretching over 13,000 miles across northern China, the Great Wall is a UNESCO World Heritage site and one of the New Seven Wonders of the World. Its history spans more than 2,000 years, with sections built and rebuilt during different dynasties. Walking on the Great Wall isn’t just a hike—it’s a journey through time.

But beyond history, the Wall offers some of the most stunning landscapes you’ll ever see. Imagine standing on a watchtower, looking out at rolling mountains covered in lush greenery or dusted with snow, with the serpent-like wall winding endlessly into the horizon. It’s a view worth every step.

Choosing the Right Section of the Great Wall

One of the biggest mistakes travelers make is assuming “the Great Wall” is one single destination. In reality, there are multiple sections near Beijing, each with its own personality, crowd level, and difficulty. Picking the right one based on your interest, fitness level, and time is key to a great experience.

Badaling: The Most Popular Section

If it’s your first time, you’ve probably seen pictures of Badaling. It’s the most restored and most visited section, with cable cars, souvenir shops, and modern facilities. Because it’s easily accessible (about 1.5 hours from Beijing), it can get very crowded, especially on weekends and holidays. Go early in the morning to avoid the worst of the crowds.

Mutianyu: Family-Friendly and Scenic

Mutianyu is a fantastic balance between accessibility and beauty. It’s less crowded than Badaling but still offers cable cars, a toboggan slide down, and well-preserved walls. The views here are breathtaking, with watchtowers dotting the forested hills. It’s about a 2-hour drive from central Beijing.

Jiankou: For the Adventurous Traveler

If you’re fit and love hiking, Jiankou is the wilder, more rugged side of the Great Wall. It’s largely unrestored, with steep, crumbling paths and photogenic ruins. This section is not for beginners or those with mobility issues, but the raw, dramatic scenery is unbeatable. You’ll need proper hiking shoes and plenty of water.

Jinshanling: The Best for a Longer Hike

Jinshanling is perfect for those who want a longer, more immersive hike without the crowds. Part restored and part wild, it offers a mix of challenging terrain and iconic views. Many visitors hike from Jinshanling to Simatai (though note Simatai is currently closed for renovation). It’s further from Beijing (about 2.5 hours), so it’s often quieter.

How to Get to the Great Wall from Beijing

Transportation is where many travelers get stuck. While taxis and private drivers are an option, they can be expensive. Luckily, there are easier and budget-friendly ways.

By Tourist Bus

Many hotels and hostels in Beijing offer organized day tours to the Great Wall. These usually include transportation, entrance fees, and sometimes a guide. It’s hassle-free, especially if you’re short on time or prefer not to deal with logistics. Just be sure to read reviews—some budget tours make unwanted stops at souvenir shops or restaurants.

By Public Bus

For the independent traveler, public buses are a cheap and authentic option. From Dongzhimen Transportation Hub, Bus 916 Express will take you to Huairou, from where you can take a local minibus to Mutianyu. For Badaling, you can take a direct bus from Deshengmen Bus Station. It’s cost-effective but requires patience and some flexibility with time.

By High-Speed Train

Yes, you can take a train to the Great Wall! The high-speed rail from Beijing North Station to Badaling is a game-changer. In just 20-30 minutes, you arrive at Badaling Station, which is a short shuttle ride from the entrance. It’s fast, comfortable, and avoids Beijing’s traffic.

By Private Car or Ride-Hailing

Using a ride-hailing app like Didi (China’s Uber) or hiring a private driver for the day is convenient, especially for groups or families. You can leave on your own schedule, choose which section to visit, and have a comfortable ride back. It’s more expensive than public options, but the flexibility can be worth it.

Best Time to Visit the Great Wall

Timing is everything. The Great Wall is open year-round, but each season offers a different experience.

Spring (April to June) brings blooming flowers and comfortable temperatures, though it can be windy. Autumn (September to November) is arguably the best time—clear skies, cool weather, and beautiful fall colors. Summer (July and August) is hot, humid, and crowded with tourists. If you visit in summer, go early and bring plenty of water. Winter (December to February) is cold and sometimes snowy, but the wall covered in snow is magical—and the crowds are thin. Just be cautious of icy paths.

Essential Tips for a Smooth Visit

  • Wear comfortable shoes with good grip. The steps are often uneven and steep.
  • Bring water and snacks. While there are vendors at popular sections, prices are inflated.
  • Sun protection is a must—hat, sunglasses, and sunscreen. There’s little shade on the wall.
  • Carry cash. Some entrances and local transport may not accept credit cards or mobile payments.
  • Don’t rush. Take your time, enjoy the views, and soak in the history.

Beyond the Wall: Combining Your Trip with Local Experiences

Make a day of it! Many sections of the Great Wall are near rural villages where you can experience authentic Chinese culture. Stop by a local farmhouse for a home-cooked meal—try dishes like nongjia cai (farmhouse-style vegetables) or freshly made jiaozi (dumplings). Some areas also offer homestays if you want to wake up to stunning wall views.

If you’re interested in history, consider visiting the Ming Tombs on the way back to Beijing, which are the burial sites of Ming Dynasty emperors. Or, pair your trip with a visit to the Summer Palace for a full day of imperial exploration.

Photography Tips for Stunning Great Wall Shots

The Great Wall is a photographer’s dream. For the best light, go early in the morning or late in the afternoon. The golden hour casts a beautiful glow on the ancient stones. Use a wide-angle lens to capture the wall winding through the mountains, and don’t forget to include some of the watchtowers for scale and context. For a unique perspective, fly a drone (where permitted) to get aerial shots of this architectural marvel.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Underestimating the physical challenge. Even restored sections involve climbing steep steps.
  • Not checking the weather. The wall can be foggy or rainy, obscuring views.
  • Assuming all sections are the same. Research and choose based on your preferences.
  • Forgetting your passport. Some sections require it for ticket purchase.

The Great Wall is a must-see, and with these tips, your visit can be as smooth as it is memorable. Whether you choose the convenience of Badaling, the beauty of Mutianyu, or the adventure of Jiankou, you’re in for an experience of a lifetime.
