A First-Timer’s Guide to Ordering Peking Duck in Beijing

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

There’s nothing quite like the crispy, golden skin and succulent meat of Peking Duck—a dish that has become synonymous with Beijing’s culinary identity. For first-time visitors, ordering this iconic dish can feel both exciting and slightly intimidating. Where do you go? How do you eat it? And what’s the proper way to enjoy it without committing a culinary faux pas? This guide will walk you through everything you need to know to savor Peking Duck like a local.

Why Peking Duck is a Must-Try

Peking Duck (北京烤鸭, Běijīng kǎoyā) isn’t just food—it’s an experience. With a history dating back to the imperial kitchens of the Ming Dynasty, this dish is a masterpiece of roasting techniques, precision carving, and interactive dining. The duck is traditionally roasted in a wood-fired oven, resulting in skin so crisp it shatters at the touch, paired with tender, flavorful meat.

For travelers, trying Peking Duck is a rite of passage. It’s a dish that embodies Beijing’s rich culture, and no trip to the city is complete without indulging in this culinary legend.

Where to Order the Best Peking Duck

Not all Peking Duck is created equal. While you’ll find variations across the city, these are the top spots that consistently deliver an unforgettable experience:

1. Quanjude (全聚德)

The most famous name in Peking Duck, Quanjude has been serving royalty and tourists alike since 1864. Their ducks are roasted over fruitwood, giving them a distinct smoky aroma. Expect a slightly touristy but authentic experience.

2. Da Dong (大董)

A modern twist on tradition, Da Dong is known for its leaner ducks and artistic presentation. Their "super lean" roast duck is a hit among health-conscious diners.

3. Bianyifang (便宜坊)

One of Beijing’s oldest duck restaurants, Bianyifang uses a closed-oven method, resulting in incredibly juicy meat. A great alternative if you prefer a less touristy vibe.

4. Siji Minfu (四季民福)

A favorite among locals, Siji Minfu offers a more relaxed setting without compromising on quality. Their duck is consistently excellent, and the views (especially at their Forbidden City branch) are unbeatable.

How to Order Peking Duck Like a Pro

Walking into a Peking Duck restaurant can be overwhelming, especially if you don’t speak Mandarin. Here’s a step-by-step breakdown of what to expect:

Step 1: Choosing the Duck

Most restaurants will ask how many people are dining to determine the size of the duck. A whole duck typically serves 2-4 people. If you’re dining solo, some places offer half-duck options.

Step 2: Watching the Carving Ritual

Once your duck is ready, a chef will often carve it tableside—a performance in itself. The skin is meticulously separated from the meat, sliced into thin pieces, and arranged beautifully on a plate.

Step 3: Assembling Your Wrap

The classic way to eat Peking Duck is by wrapping the crispy skin and meat in a thin pancake (báobǐng) with hoisin sauce, sliced cucumbers, and scallions. Some places also offer sugar or garlic paste as additional condiments.

Step 4: Exploring Other Parts of the Duck

Don’t miss out on the rest of the duck! Many restaurants serve the remaining meat in stir-fries, soups, or even as a second course with different flavors.

Etiquette Tips for Eating Peking Duck

To avoid any accidental missteps, keep these unwritten rules in mind:

  • Don’t overload your pancake—less is more. Overstuffing makes it hard to wrap neatly.
  • Use chopsticks gracefully—some locals use their hands, but chopsticks are the safer bet in formal settings.
  • Try the skin first—the crispiest bites are best enjoyed before they cool down.
  • Don’t waste the bones—some restaurants make a flavorful soup from them.

Pairing Peking Duck with the Perfect Drink

While tea is the traditional accompaniment, modern diners often enjoy Peking Duck with:

  • Baijiu (白酒) – A strong Chinese liquor that cuts through the richness.
  • Dry Riesling or Pinot Noir – If you prefer wine, these pair beautifully with the duck’s flavors.
  • Jasmine Tea – A light, fragrant option that cleanses the palate.

Beyond the Duck: Other Must-Try Dishes

While Peking Duck is the star, a full Beijing dining experience includes these supporting acts:

  • Zhajiangmian (炸酱面) – Hand-pulled noodles with savory soybean paste.
  • Hot and Sour Soup (酸辣汤) – A tangy, spicy broth with tofu and mushrooms.
  • Scallion Pancakes (葱油饼) – Crispy, flaky, and perfect for soaking up sauces.

Insider Tips for the Best Experience

  • Book ahead – Top spots like Quanjude and Da Dong often have long waits.
  • Go for lunch – Dinners can be crowded; lunch offers a more relaxed pace.
  • Ask for recommendations – Servers often know the best way to enjoy their signature dish.

Peking Duck isn’t just a meal—it’s a celebration of Beijing’s history, craftsmanship, and love for good food. Whether you’re a first-timer or a seasoned traveler, each bite is a taste of tradition. So grab a pancake, pile on the crispy skin, and savor one of the world’s greatest culinary treasures.
