Worthwhile Destinations Around Beijing

  • Home
  • Travel Blog
  • Blog Details

Beijing, the capital of China, is a city full of rich history, ancient architecture, and vibrant culture. However, the surrounding areas of Beijing also offer some incredible attractions that are worth exploring. From ancient cities to picturesque landscapes, there are many destinations near Beijing that provide a unique and fascinating glimpse into China’s history and natural beauty. Whether you're looking to escape the hustle and bustle of the city or explore some hidden gems, the areas around Beijing have something for everyone.

1. The Great Wall of China – Mutianyu Section

The Great Wall of China is undoubtedly one of the most iconic landmarks in the world, and one of the most famous sections is located just outside of Beijing. The Mutianyu Section is a less crowded but incredibly scenic part of the Great Wall, making it a popular choice for visitors who want to experience the grandeur of the wall without the heavy tourist traffic found at the more famous Badaling Section.

The Mutianyu Great Wall is well-preserved and offers some of the best panoramic views of the surrounding mountains and valleys. Visitors can hike along the wall or take a cable car up to the top for an easier experience. Whether you are an avid hiker or someone who simply wants to marvel at the impressive structure, Mutianyu is a must-visit destination just an hour or two from Beijing.

2. The Summer Palace (Yiheyuan)

Located about 15 kilometers northwest of Beijing, the Summer Palace (Yiheyuan) is a vast imperial garden and one of the most beautiful places to visit in Beijing's outskirts. The palace complex is set around Kunming Lake, and its meticulously landscaped grounds, pavilions, and temples create a serene and picturesque atmosphere. The Summer Palace was once the royal retreat for Chinese emperors, offering a peaceful escape from the bustling city.

Visitors can stroll through the lovely gardens, take a boat ride on Kunming Lake, or climb up to the top of Longevity Hill to enjoy breathtaking views of the surrounding area. The Summer Palace is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and offers an immersive experience of China’s imperial history, architecture, and landscaping.

3. The Ming Tombs

The Ming Tombs are located around 50 kilometers north of Beijing and are the burial sites of 13 emperors of the Ming Dynasty, one of China’s most important dynasties. The tombs are scattered across a picturesque valley and are surrounded by ancient forests and mountains. Each tomb is a grand mausoleum, and the site as a whole is a fascinating journey into Chinese history and culture.

The tombs are accessible by a scenic walk along the Sacred Way, a path lined with stone statues of animals and officials that once guarded the tombs. The most famous tombs at the site include the Chang Ling Tomb, the largest and most well-preserved, and the Ding Ling Tomb, which contains the remains of the Emperor Wanli.

4. The Ancient City of Jinshanling

The Jinshanling Ancient City is located about two hours northeast of Beijing and offers a unique experience for history buffs and nature lovers alike. Known for its well-preserved ancient architecture and stunning mountain views, the city is a hidden gem that offers a quieter alternative to the more popular tourist destinations.

Jinshanling is famous for its ancient city wall, which was built during the Ming Dynasty. The wall is less touristy and provides a peaceful atmosphere for exploration. Visitors can explore the historic city, wander through ancient streets and alleys, and visit various historical landmarks, all while surrounded by beautiful natural scenery.

5. Chengde Mountain Resort

Located about 230 kilometers northeast of Beijing, the Chengde Mountain Resort (Chengde Biyunshan) is a former imperial summer retreat that was used by the Qing Dynasty emperors. This vast palace complex is set against the backdrop of beautiful mountains and lush forests, making it one of China’s most beautiful historic sites.

Chengde Mountain Resort consists of several gardens, pavilions, temples, and a massive artificial lake, all designed to create a relaxing atmosphere for the royal family. Visitors can explore the beautiful grounds, visit the temples, or take a boat ride around the lake. The resort is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and is known for its historical significance as well as its scenic beauty.

6. The Longqing Gorge

Around 80 kilometers north of Beijing, Longqing Gorge is a popular destination for nature lovers and those looking to escape the city’s hustle and bustle. This picturesque gorge is filled with crystal-clear water, lush greenery, and dramatic mountain cliffs. The area is known for its impressive karst landscapes, beautiful waterfalls, and a peaceful atmosphere that makes it perfect for hiking and photography.

Visitors can take a boat ride along the river or explore the various hiking trails that lead to panoramic viewpoints. The Longqing Gorge is especially famous for its Longqing Gorge Ice Festival, held every winter, where the gorge is illuminated with colorful lights, making for a magical winter wonderland.

7. The City of Datong

Located about four hours from Beijing, Datong is an ancient city that is home to several impressive historical landmarks. The Yungang Grottoes are perhaps the most famous attraction in Datong, featuring thousands of Buddhist statues and carvings that date back to the 5th and 6th centuries. The Yungang Grottoes are a UNESCO World Heritage Site and are an essential visit for anyone interested in Chinese religious art.

In addition to the grottoes, Datong also has the impressive Hanging Monastery, which is built into a cliffside, offering visitors a unique glimpse into ancient Chinese architecture. The city itself is rich in history, and exploring its ancient temples, old city walls, and historical sites is a rewarding experience.

8. The City of Tianjin

Just a 30-minute train ride from Beijing, Tianjin is a modern, cosmopolitan city that has a fascinating mix of Western and Chinese influences. Once a major international port, Tianjin has a rich colonial history that is reflected in its architecture, with a blend of European-style buildings alongside traditional Chinese structures.

Tianjin offers a variety of attractions, including the Ancient Culture Street, where visitors can find traditional Chinese goods, the Tianjin Eye, a giant Ferris wheel offering spectacular views of the city, and the Five Great Avenues, a street lined with historic European-style buildings. Tianjin is perfect for those who want to explore a dynamic, modern Chinese city with a historical twist.

9. Huanghua Cheng Great Wall

While the most famous sections of the Great Wall may be overrun with tourists, the Huanghua Cheng section of the wall is one of the least visited, making it an ideal destination for those who want to experience the Great Wall in a more tranquil and less commercialized setting. This section, located about 70 kilometers from Beijing, is an untouched part of the wall that has not been restored, providing a more authentic and rugged experience.

Visitors can hike along the wall and enjoy breathtaking views of the surrounding mountains and valleys. This section of the wall is also famous for its Huanghua Cheng Reservoir, which reflects the wall in its clear water, creating a striking and picturesque scene.

10. The City of Pingyao

A UNESCO World Heritage Site, Pingyao is an ancient walled city located about five hours from Beijing by train. Pingyao offers visitors a unique opportunity to experience traditional Chinese architecture and culture, as the city has been incredibly well-preserved and has retained much of its old charm.

Walking through the ancient streets of Pingyao feels like stepping back in time, with old-style buildings, temples, and ancient shops. Visitors can also explore Pingyao Ancient Government Office, which offers insight into the city’s historical administration, as well as the Shuanglin Temple, which is known for its impressive collection of ancient Buddhist statues.

The surrounding area of Pingyao is filled with historic villages, temples, and ancient buildings that are perfect for those looking to explore China’s rich history in a peaceful, less touristy setting.

Beijing may be the focal point of your China adventure, but the surrounding areas offer a wealth of historical, cultural, and natural treasures that are worth exploring. Whether you’re interested in ancient history, picturesque landscapes, or charming old cities, there are plenty of destinations around Beijing that will leave a lasting impression.
